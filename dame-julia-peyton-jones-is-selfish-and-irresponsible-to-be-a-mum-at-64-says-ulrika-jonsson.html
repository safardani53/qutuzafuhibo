ABOUT ten years ago, I was in the process of giving motherhood very serious consideration for a fourth and final time.

It wasn’t that I was lacking in broodiness or unwilling to bring another child into the world — the previous three had been rather successful — but my age concerned me.

I was about to turn 40 and the prospect of being pregnant seemed fraught with risks. I was also questioning my energy levels.

Imagine my shock, then, when I learned that this week Dame Julia Peyton-Jones has become a mother for the first time at age 64.

To say I was staggered would be an under-statement.

This dame, currently living on the west coast of the US, made my contemplations a decade ago look like child’s play.

Apparently, by modern standards, 40 is positively pre-school in the motherhood stakes.

On a more profound note though, it’s very hard to get your head around someone becoming a mother at an age which is not only well past the menopause but is just a grey hair short of a pension.

Bodies aren't designed to have babies in 60s

It reeks of selfishness — an utter disregard for the fact that the child brought into the world (whether by IVF or surrogate) may not have a mother around for many years due to her old age.

I sincerely hope that Dame Julia enjoys a long life, for her daughter Pia’s sake, but what are the chances?

I accept that any of us can drop dead at a moment’s notice — but it is less likely when you’re in your thirties or forties.

I’ve tried to get my head around wanting a child at that age. I’m menopausal now and finally — as I approach my 50th — my maternal desires have vanished. I no longer look at a pregnant woman or a newborn and coo with longing and hope.

There is a very good reason for that — it’s biological.

Our bodies are not designed to have babies in our 60s, and even if this child was conceived via a surrogate, it does not change the fact that women’s falling fertility as we pass 35 is because we are not as capable of coping with the demands of motherhood.

At the age of 49, with my youngest aged eight-and-a-half, I fully admit that I don’t have the energy (and perhaps even patience) that I had when I had my firstborn at 27.

But perhaps not everyone is a hands-on mum. Some people have people who “do” motherhood for them.

How, for example, will this 64-year-old woman be able to run after a toddler when her own joints might be aching and creaking? Will little Pia be forced to push her mother around in a wheelchair when she’s ten because Mummy struggles with her mobility?

IVF has done amazing things for women’s fertility. Women are able to conceive well into their 40s, if they have decided to hold off to climb the career ladder or struggled to conceive for years.

I think it’s truly amazing what is possible. But just because we can, doesn’t mean we should.

I’ve heard it said that the end of natural pregnancy for women is 53, which seems very old to me.

Britain’s oldest mum is believed to Sue Tollefsen, who gave birth at 57 to daughter Freya. But 64 is considerably older.

And whether by natural or surrogate means, the end product remains the same.

Some might argue that there are double standards at play here when it is acceptable for Rolling Stone Ronnie Wood to father twins with wife Sally Humphreys just before his 69th birthday.

Well, for the record, I don’t think it is. But an early biology lesson will confirm that his “input” is considerably less than a woman who carries a baby.

And while I’m sure Ronnie is an amazing dad, I can’t see him getting up for night feeds and running his wee bairns around the park every afternoon.

Apart from anything else, he can pay staff to do that.

No, I feel becoming a mother at 64 is not only selfish but irresponsible.

We need to take our cue from nature

And that goes for all those who facilitated this.

Just like the IVF unit in Romania that made Adriana Iliescu a mum in 2005 at the age of 66 (and 230 days) is nothing if not, irresponsible.

Because we can — and can charge for it — doesn’t mean we should show a disregard for the physical and societal implications for the mothers’ and babies’ lives.

I find it sad that a woman should consider motherhood for the first time in her sixties.

Whatever the reasons may be for why it hadn’t happened before, they do not make the possibility and medical advancement any more acceptable or palatable.

What is needed is a sobering conversation with oneself to acknowledge that we need to take our cue from nature.

Motherhood is not a God-given right, nor is it an ageless possibility.
